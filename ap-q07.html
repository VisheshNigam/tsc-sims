Q7 Oscillations — Pendulum on the Moon
A simple pendulum has period T on Earth. The same pendulum is taken to the Moon, where g_Moon ≈ g/6. What is its new period?
✓ Correct Answer
D — T_Moon = √6 · T_Earth ≈ 2.45 T
💡 Don't Fall For This
The "linear-with-g" trap (option C): if you assumed T scales linearly with 1/g, you would get T → 6T. But the period equation has a square root: T = 2π√(L/g). Smaller g still makes T longer, but only by a factor of √6, not 6.

The "wrong direction" traps (options A and B): a smaller g means a weaker restoring force, so the pendulum swings more slowly — the period must be longer, not shorter. Any answer less than T (A or B) gets the physics backwards.

The clean ratio: T_Moon / T_Earth = √(g_Earth / g_Moon) = √6 ≈ 2.45. So if T = 2 s on Earth, the same pendulum takes about 4.9 s on the Moon.
