Q10 Oscillations — Amplitude & Period
A mass on a spring oscillates with amplitude A and period T. The same spring and mass are now released from amplitude 2A. What is the new period?
✓ Correct Answer
A — T is unchanged. T = 2π·√(m/k) — no amplitude dependence.
💡 Don't Fall For This
The "more distance, more time" trap (options B and C): students reason that with twice the amplitude, the mass has to travel twice as far per cycle, so it should take longer. But it also moves faster at every point — exactly fast enough that the two effects cancel out. The period stays the same.

The "more force, faster" trap (option D): some students reason that pulling the spring further makes it pull back harder (F = −kx is bigger), so the mass should accelerate more and finish a cycle quicker. The acceleration is bigger, but so is the distance to cover. Again, the two cancel exactly.

Why amplitude doesn't matter: T = 2π·√(m/k). Only the mass and the spring constant appear. This is the defining feature of simple harmonic motion — the period is isochronous. Galileo discovered this watching a chandelier swing in Pisa, regardless of how widely it swung.
