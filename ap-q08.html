Q8 Fluids — Continuity Equation
Water flows through a horizontal pipe that narrows from 6 cm² to 2 cm². If the speed in the wide section is 1 m/s, what is the speed in the narrow section?
✓ Correct Answer
C — v₂ = (A₁/A₂)·v₁ = (6/2)·1 = 3 m/s
💡 Don't Fall For This
The "inverted ratio" trap (option A): students who write v₂ = (A₂/A₁)·v₁ get 1·(2/6) = 0.33 m/s. That's backwards — it slows the water down at the narrow section, which is the opposite of what we observe (think of squeezing a hose).

The "speed doesn't change" trap (option B): if you assumed the same flow speed regardless of pipe area, you'd pick 1 m/s. But the same volume of water has to pass through every cross-section per second, so it must speed up where the pipe narrows.

The "wrong factor" trap (option D): some students multiply by total area sum or do another arithmetic slip, landing on 6 m/s. The clean ratio is 3:1, so the speed triples.

Answer to unlock
Wide section (A₁ = 6 cm²)
v₁ = 1.0 m/s
Slower flow because there's more cross-section for the same volume rate.
Narrow section (A₂ = 2 cm²)
v₂ = 3.0 m/s
Same fluid, smaller pipe — speed must triple to keep flow rate constant.
Wide section area (A₁): 6.0 cm²
A₁·v₁ = A₂·v₂  ⟹  v₂ = (A₁/A₂)·v₁ = (6/2)(1) = 3 m/s
Watch the particles: same number per second pass each cross-section, but they have to move faster through the narrow part to keep up. That's continuity — volume flow rate (A·v) is conserved.
